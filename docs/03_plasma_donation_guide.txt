PLASMA DONATION — DONOR INFORMATION GUIDE
Version 2.1 | Updated March 2025

========================================
WHAT IS PLASMA?
========================================

Plasma is the straw-coloured liquid component of blood that makes up approximately 55% of total blood volume. It carries proteins, hormones, clotting factors, and antibodies. Australian Red Cross Lifeblood collects plasma primarily to manufacture life-saving medicines including:

  - Immunoglobulin (Ig) therapies — used to treat immune deficiencies and autoimmune conditions.
  - Albumin — used in burns treatment and liver disease.
  - Clotting factor concentrates — used in haemophilia management.
  - Hyperimmune immunoglobulins — e.g., tetanus Ig, varicella-zoster Ig.

Australia is one of the few countries in the world that has committed to self-sufficiency in plasma-derived medicines, meaning Australian patients are treated with Australian plasma.

========================================
THE PLASMAPHERESIS PROCESS
========================================

Plasma is collected by a process called PLASMAPHERESIS (also called apheresis). Unlike whole blood donation, apheresis uses a machine to separate the plasma from the other blood components, which are then returned to the donor.

Step-by-step:

  1. REGISTRATION & HEALTH CHECK (approx. 15 min)
     Staff check your identity, review your health questionnaire, and perform a fingerprick haemoglobin test. Blood pressure and pulse are also recorded.

  2. VENEPUNCTURE (approx. 5 min)
     A sterile needle is inserted into a vein in the arm — usually the antecubital vein at the elbow.

  3. APHERESIS MACHINE CYCLE (approx. 35–45 min)
     Blood is drawn into the apheresis machine in small aliquots (roughly 30–50 mL at a time). The machine centrifuges the blood, separates and collects the plasma into a sterile collection bag, and returns the red cells and platelets to the donor through the same needle.

  4. RECOVERY & REFRESHMENTS (approx. 15 min)
     After the needle is removed, the donor rests and has a drink and a snack to aid recovery.

Total visit time: approximately 60–75 minutes.

========================================
HOW MUCH PLASMA IS COLLECTED?
========================================

The volume of plasma collected depends on the donor's body weight:

  Weight 50–59 kg   →  570 mL collected
  Weight 60–69 kg   →  655 mL collected
  Weight 70–79 kg   →  740 mL collected
  Weight 80–89 kg   →  825 mL collected
  Weight 90+ kg     →  880 mL collected

The body replaces donated plasma within approximately 24–48 hours, which is why plasma can be donated more frequently than whole blood.

========================================
ELIGIBILITY SPECIFIC TO PLASMA DONATION
========================================

In addition to general donor eligibility:

  - Donors must have a protein level adequate for plasma (total protein >= 60 g/L). A protein test is performed periodically.
  - Donors who have received immunoglobulin (Ig) therapy in the past must be deferred (Ig therapy deferred 12 months).
  - Female donors who have been pregnant: Plasma from previously pregnant women may contain HLA antibodies, which can cause Transfusion-Related Acute Lung Injury (TRALI) in recipients. Lifeblood assesses each case; many previously pregnant donors can still donate plasma after antibody screening.

========================================
DONATION FREQUENCY
========================================

Plasma may be donated every 2 weeks (14 days), up to a maximum of 26 donations per year. This higher frequency compared to whole blood is safe because the body replenishes plasma rapidly.

Lifeblood encourages plasma donors to book regular fortnightly appointments to help maintain a consistent supply for manufacturers.

========================================
SIDE EFFECTS AND SAFETY
========================================

Plasma donation is considered very safe. Common minor side effects include:

  - Tingling or numbness in the lips or fingertips (caused by citrate, an anticoagulant added to the blood during apheresis — this usually resolves quickly).
  - Mild fatigue directly after donation.
  - Slight bruising or soreness at the needle site.

Donors are encouraged to:
  - Drink at least 500 mL of water in the 2 hours before donation.
  - Avoid fatty or high-lipid meals for 4 hours before donation, as high blood lipid levels (lipaemia) can render plasma unusable.
  - Rest for at least 15 minutes after donation before leaving.

Serious adverse events (vasovagal syncope, haematoma, nerve injury) are rare and managed by trained staff on site.
