========================================
WHAT ARE PLATELETS?
========================================

Platelets (thrombocytes) are tiny, colourless cell fragments in the blood whose primary role is to form clots and stop bleeding. They are produced in the bone marrow and have a lifespan of only 7–10 days in the body.

In a clinical context, platelet transfusions are used for:

  - Patients with thrombocytopenia (dangerously low platelet count) — common in leukaemia, lymphoma, and other haematological cancers, or as a side effect of chemotherapy.
  - Patients undergoing major surgery who have low platelet counts.
  - Patients with platelet function disorders (e.g., Glanzmann thrombasthenia, Bernard-Soulier syndrome).
  - Trauma and massive haemorrhage protocols.

One platelet donation from a single apheresis procedure can provide enough platelets to treat one adult cancer patient for an entire day of chemotherapy.

========================================
THE PLATELET DONATION PROCESS
========================================

Like plasma, platelets are collected by apheresis.

  1. REGISTRATION & HEALTH SCREENING (approx. 15 min)
     Includes identity verification, health questionnaire, fingerprick haemoglobin test, and platelet count estimation.

     Note: Donors must have a minimum platelet count of 200 x 10^9/L to proceed with platelet donation. If the count is adequate, donation proceeds.

  2. VENEPUNCTURE (approx. 5 min)
     Sterile needle insertion, usually in the antecubital fossa.

  3. APHERESIS CYCLE (approx. 60–90 min)
     The apheresis machine processes the blood multiple times, extracting platelets with each pass. The procedure takes longer than plasma donation because platelet separation requires more cycles.

  4. RECOVERY (approx. 15 min)
     Rest, refreshments, and monitoring before departure.

Total visit time: approximately 90–120 minutes.

========================================
PLATELET SHELF LIFE — WHY REGULAR DONATIONS MATTER
========================================

Unlike red blood cells (which can be stored for up to 42 days) or frozen plasma (stored for up to 2 years), platelets have an extremely short shelf life:

  Storage temperature:  20–24 degrees Celsius (room temperature, with agitation)
  Maximum storage:      7 days from collection (in Australia, hospitals typically use within 5 days)

This very short shelf life means that a consistent, frequent supply of platelet donors is essential. There can be no "stockpiling" of platelets — the supply chain must be continuous.

========================================
IRRADIATED PLATELETS
========================================

Certain patient groups — including bone marrow transplant recipients, patients with Hodgkin's lymphoma, and severely immunocompromised individuals — must receive IRRADIATED blood products. Irradiation inactivates donor T-lymphocytes that contaminate platelet concentrates, preventing Transfusion-Associated Graft-versus-Host Disease (TA-GvHD), which can be fatal.

Lifeblood irradiates platelet units on request from hospital blood banks. Irradiation does not affect platelet function.

========================================
ELIGIBILITY SPECIFIC TO PLATELET DONATION
========================================

  - Minimum platelet count: 200 x 10^9/L (checked at each visit).
  - Donors must NOT have taken aspirin or ibuprofen in the 3 days before donation. These medications impair platelet function. Paracetamol is acceptable.
  - Donors must NOT have taken any anticoagulant medication (e.g., warfarin, rivaroxaban, dabigatran) in the 14 days prior.
  - Frequency: Every 2 weeks, maximum 24 times per year.

========================================
WHAT HAPPENS TO PLATELET DONATIONS?
========================================

After collection, platelet units are:
  1. Tested for bacterial contamination using aerobic and anaerobic culture.
  2. ABO and Rh typed.
  3. Leukodepleted (white cells removed by filtration to reduce febrile non-haemolytic reactions and CMV transmission).
  4. Stored in agitators at 20–24°C until issued to hospitals.

Each apheresis platelet unit collected from a single donor is equivalent in therapeutic dose to approximately 4–6 whole-blood-derived platelet concentrates (pooled from multiple donors). Single-donor (apheresis) platelets are therefore preferred to reduce the number of donor exposures for a single patient.
