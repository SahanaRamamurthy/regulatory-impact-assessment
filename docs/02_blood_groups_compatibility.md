# Blood Groups and Compatibility Reference
**Australian Red Cross Lifeblood — Clinical Reference Sheet**

---

## 1. The ABO Blood Group System

The ABO system is the most clinically significant blood group classification. It is determined by the presence or absence of A and B antigens on the surface of red blood cells, and corresponding antibodies in plasma.

| Blood Group | Antigens on RBC | Antibodies in Plasma | Can Donate To | Can Receive From |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | A | Anti-B | A, AB | A, O |
| B | B | Anti-A | B, AB | B, O |
| AB | A and B | None | AB only | A, B, AB, O |
| O | None | Anti-A and Anti-B | A, B, AB, O | O only |

### 1.1 Universal Donors and Recipients
- **O negative (O−)** is the universal red cell donor. It can be transfused to any patient regardless of ABO/Rh status — critical in emergency situations where the patient's blood type is unknown.
- **AB positive (AB+)** is the universal plasma donor.
- **AB positive** recipients can receive red cells from any ABO/Rh group.

## 2. The Rhesus (Rh) System

The Rh system is the second most clinically important blood group system. The D antigen (RhD) is the most significant.

- **RhD positive**: RhD antigen is present on red cells (approximately 83% of Australians).
- **RhD negative**: RhD antigen is absent (approximately 17% of Australians).

### 2.1 Rh Incompatibility in Pregnancy
If an RhD-negative mother carries an RhD-positive foetus, sensitisation can occur — the mother's immune system develops Anti-D antibodies. In subsequent pregnancies, these antibodies can cross the placenta and destroy foetal red cells, causing **Haemolytic Disease of the Foetus and Newborn (HDFN)**.

**Prevention:** Anti-D immunoglobulin (RhD prophylaxis) is administered to RhD-negative women:
- At 28 and 34 weeks gestation (routine antenatal prophylaxis).
- Within 72 hours after any potentially sensitising event (delivery, miscarriage, amniocentesis, etc.).

## 4. Other Clinically Significant Blood Group Systems

Beyond ABO and Rh, there are over 40 internationally recognised blood group systems. The following are most relevant to transfusion practice in Australia:

- **Kell (K)**: Anti-K is the third most common cause of haemolytic transfusion reactions. K-negative blood is preferred for females of childbearing potential.
- **Kidd (Jk)**: Anti-Jk<sup>a</sup> and Anti-Jk<sup>b</sup> are notorious for causing delayed haemolytic transfusion reactions.
- **Duffy (Fy)**: The Duffy antigen is the receptor for *Plasmodium vivax* malaria. Fy(a−b−) individuals have natural resistance to *P. vivax*.
- **MNS system**: Includes the S and s antigens; anti-S and anti-s can cause severe haemolytic reactions.

## 5. Electronic Crossmatch vs. Serological Crossmatch

| Method | Indication | Turnaround |
|---|---|---|
| Electronic crossmatch | Routine; patient has valid group and screen | ~5 minutes |
| Immediate spin crossmatch | Urgent; no previous sample | ~15 minutes |
| Full serological crossmatch | Alloantibody detected; complex history | 30–60 minutes |

---

## 6. Neonatal Transfusion Considerations

- Neonates (< 4 months) are transfused with **irradiated, CMV-negative, leukodepleted** red cells.
- Group O, RhD-negative red cells are used when the baby's group is unknown.
- Blood must be less than **5 days old** to minimise potassium load.
